# Compensation Philosophy & Levels

_How Focus sets pay, level-by-level expectations for every practice, bonus pillars, and the Focus non-negotiables._

> **Draft (Not Yet Final):** This page is an internal working draft. Salary figures are illustrative placeholders and will be updated before this content is finalized. Do not share outside Focus.

## Comp Philosophy

- **One level = one salary number.** No negotiation, no ranges.
- **Level is tied directly to total years of professional experience** in the discipline.
- **Annual COLA adjustments** are applied across the entire grid.
- **Bonuses are the mechanism for rewarding impact**, not base salary adjustments.
- **W-2 status is required.** Development is not optional.

### How We Derived Salary Targets

Each level’s number is set from market data, not negotiation. We benchmark against public salary data for comparable roles in civic tech and government services (including peer firms, federal labor-rate data, and published pay bands) and then map those benchmarks to our years-of-experience bands. We target a single competitive number per level rather than a range, so two people at the same level with the same experience are always paid the same. The grid is reviewed annually: COLA adjustments apply across every level, and benchmarks are re-checked so the grid stays competitive as the market moves.

Sources we benchmark against:

- **Federal labor-rate (LCAT) data:** [GSA Contract-Awarded Labor Category (CALC) pricing data](https://calc.gsa.gov/), which shows awarded hourly rates by labor category and experience level across GSA schedule contracts
- **Federal wage statistics:** [BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS)](https://www.bls.gov/oes/) for software developers, designers, and project management occupations, including percentile breakdowns by metro area
- **Federal pay scales:** [OPM GS pay tables](https://www.opm.gov/policy-data-oversight/pay-leave/salaries-wages/), as a reference point for the government-side equivalents of our roles
- **Civic tech peer firms with published pay:** posted salary bands from comparable firms, including [Nava PBC](https://www.navapbc.com/careers), [Ad Hoc](https://adhoc.team/careers/), and [Truss](https://truss.works/jobs), and the [GSA TTS (18F) handbook](https://github.com/GSA-TTS/handbook) pay structure

## How Levels Work

Every practice uses the same ten levels and the same naming convention: **Associate → I → II → Senior → Staff → Principal → Director**. Levels are pegged to total years of professional experience in the discipline, and the bands widen as you go up — because a year of experience changes what someone can do dramatically early in a career, and much less later on.

- **One-year steps through Associate and I** (levels 1–4, years 0–4): juniors move up a level every year while growth is fastest
- **Two-year steps from II through Staff** (levels 5–8, years 4–12)
- **Principal is the three-year capstone of the IC arc** (level 9, years 12–15)
- **Director begins at 15 years** (level 10). It is conferred by leadership, not automatic. Fifteen years makes you eligible; the appointment is made when the role exists and the person is ready.

Associate, I, and II each span two levels. The expectations for the pair are continuous — the second notch is the same role practiced with more consistency and less support.

## Additional Roles

These are assignments, not promotions. They do not change base salary. They unlock eligibility for the “Helps Focus Scale” bonus pillar.

| **Manager of people** | Direct people management responsibility for Focus staff |
| **KP on contract** | Key person designation on a specific government contract |

## Bonus Pillars

Bonuses are paid twice a year. The percentage of salary increases with level. Unused pool is surfaced company-wide each cycle.

### Pillar 1: Helps Focus Scale

Biggest leg up for contract leads, managers of people, and KPs.

- Onboarding buddy program
- Interviewing
- Practice leadership

### Pillar 2: Impact

Impact is about what actually changed because you were on the work: outcomes delivered for users and clients, and contributions that help the business grow. It’s measured by results, not activity.

- Outcomes shipped that users and clients can point to
- Work that leads to expanded scope, renewals, or follow-on opportunities
- Press / case studies that demonstrate the value of the work

### Pillar 3: Builds Brand & Client Relationships

- Speaking, writing, demoing work
- Stakeholder trust
- Winning new work

### Pillar 4: Corporate Improvements

- Org health
- Process
- Change management

## Focus Non-Negotiables

These apply to every hire, all practices, all levels. One “no” ends the conversation.

- [Adaptability](#): Adjusts when ground shifts without losing forward momentum.
- [Ownership](#): Treats the outcome as theirs. Doesn’t wait to be told. Closes loops.
- [Mission alignment](#): Cares about the work itself. Makes mission-forward choices when no one’s watching.
- [Communication](#): Says the real thing, to the right audience, in the right setting. Clarity over jargon.
- [Collaboration](#): Makes the people around them better. Doesn’t hoard credit, context, or decisions.
- [Decency](#): Treats teammates, clients, and partners with respect, always. Brilliance is never a license to be corrosive.
- [Stakeholder navigation](#): Reads the room. Manages up, across, and down. Builds trust with people whose priorities differ.
- [Professionalism](#): Reliable, prepared, composed. Honors commitments. Represents Focus well in any room.
- [Conflict management](#): Pulls disagreement into the open early. Disagrees without damaging the relationship.
- [Lean / user-centered thinking](#): Anchors decisions on user value. Ships the smallest thing that proves the bet.
- [Curiosity](#): Asks better questions than the assignment requires. Follows threads on their own.
- [Growth mindset](#): Treats feedback as information, not threat. Sees being wrong as a starting point.
- [Builder orientation](#): Learns by doing. Has a track record of shipping things because building is how they think.
