# Engagement Model for Prime Contracts

## Terminology

These terms are not synonymous and should not be used interchangeably.

**Contract Leads** are accountable for the successful delivery of the contract as a whole. Contract leads are named per contract.

**Leads** refers to functional or workstream leads who own a specific discipline (e.g. research, architecture, design) but are not accountable for overall contract delivery.

"Substantial" effort, as referenced throughout this document, means roughly more than a sprint or two of work. Anything at or above that threshold requires CDO/contracts involvement before commitment, not just internal team scoping.

## Pre-Kickoff: Notice of Award Through Contract Start

This phase sits with the CDO until Focus has a dedicated program management office or practice. Once that exists, this phase transitions there.

**BD-to-Delivery Handoff Meeting.** Triggered at notice of award. BD walks the assigned key personnel through the market research that informed the proposal, the proposal itself, assumptions made during the bid, and the staffing plan as proposed. This is the one chance to surface gaps between what was promised and what's realistic before staffing decisions get made.

**Staffing and Recruiting Sync.** If new hiring is needed:

- Recruiting and the relevant practice lead(s) convene to assess current bench strength against what the contract requires.
- Job descriptions get drafted; leads have input into JDs before they're finalized.
- A pass on evaluation criteria: are we testing for mission fit, technical capability, consulting know-how, and AI fluency, not just technical skill matching. This is worth standardizing once, rather than re-deciding it per contract.

**Leads Notification and Transition Cadence.** Once key personnel are identified, they begin meeting on a defined cadence even while still on current assignments, to start transition planning early rather than waiting for the new contract's actual start date.

- If the outgoing person is on a subcontract, they coordinate with their assigned delivery lead on that program.
- If they're on a prime or JV contract, they coordinate with the head delivery person on that contract.
- For Nava subcontracts specifically, written notification goes to the Nava contracts team at two points: once the award is won, and again once the project's intended start date is confirmed. Both need to be tracked and coordinated, not just the first one.

**Contract Document Distribution.** The contract, PWS/SOW, and any special clauses get distributed to the contract leads who will be managing scope day to day, not just retained by BD and the CDO.

**Tooling and Systems Access.** Confirm access to client systems (e.g. client Jira, Confluence, Figma) is provisioned, and that Focus-side systems (time tracking, invoicing setup) are configured under the correct contract code, before kickoff, not discovered in week one.

**Compliance and Reporting Setup.** If the contract has its own performance-evaluation cycle (e.g. CPARS), invoicing cadence, or reporting requirements, that gets set up at kickoff rather than retrofitted later once already behind.

**Risk Register Initialization.** Start the program risk register at kickoff, seeded with anything flagged during BD or staffing, rather than creating it reactively after something has already gone wrong.

**Ramp-Down and Knowledge Transfer.** The outgoing team executes documentation and knowledge transfer sessions on their current contract before fully shifting to the new one, so they're not arriving at the new contract still mentally and operationally tied to the old one.

**Client Kickoff.** Ownership of leading the kickoff with the new client, on whatever date is set, sits with the contract lead team, not the CDO or CEO. By kickoff, key personnel need enough context (from the BD handoff, staffing decisions, and transition work above) to run it credibly themselves.

## CDO's Role on a Mature Prime Contract

As a prime contract matures past initial kickoff, the CDO transitions out of day-to-day delivery operations into an Account Lead role for that contract. Going forward, the CDO owns:

- All contract negotiations with the contracting officer
- Identifying and pursuing business expansion opportunities with the client
- Focus leadership representation in product demos, client stakeholder meetings, and customer-facing sessions
- Performance-evaluation perspective input (e.g. CPARS for federal contracts, or the contract's equivalent performance mechanism), injected proactively into planning conversations the CDO is not part of, particularly around performance evaluation periods
- The client relationship and accounts lead function overall

Day-to-day delivery operations transition to the named Contract Leads for that contract.

Where Focus subcontracts pieces of a prime contract out to other vendors, the CDO retains ownership of that sub-vendor relationship and its contractual terms. ## Meeting Cadence

**Monthly 1:1s.** CDO holds a monthly 1:1 with each contract lead, ongoing.

**Leads Sync.** CDO attends on a defined cadence (monthly recommended, adjust per contract). Purpose: the CDO needs program-level inputs from this sync to run client-facing status meetings effectively.

Standing items for this sync:

- **Sprint/status report check.** Have required reports been submitted on schedule, and who owns that submission.
- **Artifact check.** What new artifacts have been created, and are they on the client's required instances (e.g. client Confluence, client Jira, or contract-specified systems).
- **Risk management.** Proactive review of program risks, not just reactive flagging when something goes wrong.

## Escalation Model

Where the contract leads team has not yet been trained in contract operations or administration, anything cost-adjacent or contract-adjacent needs to come through the CDO acting as the contracts team. As leads build that capability over time, this threshold can be revisited per contract.

**Automatic escalation to the CDO/contracts, regardless of size:**

- Anything cost-adjacent
- New staffing or role additions
- Net new work requests from the customer
- Client dissatisfaction signals

**Leads own, with a first-pass recommendation brought to the CDO:**

- Scope clarity questions. First response is always to review the contract and come up with a proposed interpretation, not just a flag of uncertainty.

**Leads own outright:**

- Day-to-day delivery
- Sprint execution
- Internal prioritization within existing scope

## Contract Lifecycle Triggers: Option Years and Recompetes

Once a contract reaches steady-state operations, the CDO's involvement is intentionally lighter (per the Account Lead role above). That changes at defined lifecycle moments, when CDO involvement should temporarily increase:

- **Option year exercise window.** A defined period before the government's (or client's) option year decision point, the CDO re-engages directly with the contracting officer to confirm intent to exercise, and internally to prep the contract leads and broader team for what continuation or change looks like.
- **Recompete window.** A defined period before a recompete deadline, the CDO re-engages with BD to shape the recompete strategy, drawing on lessons and relationship context the contract leads have accumulated during steady-state delivery.

## Contract Closeout / Off-Ramp

This phase mirrors the kickoff phase and applies whether the contract ends on schedule, isn't recompeted, or Focus loses a recompete.

**Closeout Notification.** Once it's known a contract is ending (lost recompete, natural conclusion, or early termination), the CDO notifies affected contract leads and staff as early as possible, given the staffing lead times referenced elsewhere in Focus's operating model (60 days for redeployment, 90 days for reductions).

**Staff Redeployment Planning.** Practice leads and the CDO identify where outgoing staff can be redeployed to other contracts or roles before the contract ends, rather than waiting until the last week.

**Client Exit and Relationship Preservation.** The CDO manages the client-facing exit conversation, regardless of why the contract is ending, with an eye toward preserving the relationship for future business. Losing a recompete is not a reason to burn the relationship; Focus may bid again or work with the client elsewhere.

**Knowledge Transfer to Incoming Party.** Per applicable contract requirements (e.g. PWS-mandated transition-out provisions), ensure all project-specific knowledge and documentation is shared with the incoming contractor or client team before Focus rolls off.

**Asset and Access Offboarding.** Confirm Focus systems, accounts, and access tied to the contract are properly closed out (time tracking, invoicing, client system access), and that anything owed to the client (final deliverables, documentation, IP per contract terms) is delivered.

**Closeout Retrospective.** A short internal retrospective on what worked and what didn't over the life of the contract, captured for future BD and kickoff use, particularly if recompete is still a possibility down the line.

## FAQs for Contract Leads

### A team member has a performance issue. What should I do?

First, give the person direct 1:1 feedback. Be specific about what you observed and what you need to see change. If the issue continues after that conversation, escalate to their manager with documentation of the feedback given and the continued pattern. Don't escalate as a first step; the first move is always direct, specific feedback to the individual.

### The customer asks us to integrate a new tool, take on a new workstream, or work with another vendor. What should I do?

The contract leads come together first to scope the level of effort. If it's at or below the substantial threshold (roughly a sprint or two), handle it within existing team capacity. If it's substantial, that triggers a notification to the CDO/contracts to discuss a scope, timeline, or trade-off adjustment. Don't commit to substantial new work before that conversation happens.

### Another vendor, leader, or client division doesn't like what we built and doesn't think it'll work for their office. What should I do?

Set up a 1:1 call with them. As contract lead, you're expected to hear and absorb the negative feedback directly, and try to understand where they're coming from and what goals they actually have. Bring in the relevant functional lead (e.g. research, design) to help structure that session if useful. After that conversation, loop in your program team so they're aware. From there, log the request appropriately tagged by originating office, but also factor it into how the contract's product scales more broadly. If this becomes a pattern, that's a relationship risk and it comes to the CDO to handle directly.

### The client wants to do something that isn't technically sound. What should I do?

Put your objection in writing: the recommended approach, the reasons against it, and submit it to the relevant client leadership. If they push forward anyway and you're forced to proceed, raise it to the CDO so it can be communicated to the contracting officer and logged in the program risk register as a documented risk with potential outcomes. The goal is to have it on the record before, not after, something goes wrong.

### A contract lead isn't sure if something is cost-adjacent or just an operational decision. What should I do?

Default to escalating, particularly while the leads team doesn't yet have contract ops training. A quick flag costs nothing; an unflagged cost decision can.

### One contract lead disagrees with another contract lead on a delivery decision. What should I do?

Try to resolve it between the two of you first. If you can't reach alignment, bring it to the monthly leads check-in or the relevant 1:1 with the CDO rather than letting it sit unresolved or play out in front of the broader team.
