Compensation Philosophy & Levels

Comp Philosophy

  • One level = one salary number. No negotiation, no ranges.
  • Level is tied directly to total years of professional experience in the discipline.
  • Annual COLA adjustments are applied across the entire grid.
  • Bonuses are the mechanism for rewarding impact — not base salary adjustments.
  • W-2 status is required. Development is not optional.

How We Derived Salary Targets

Each level’s number is set from market data, not negotiation. We benchmark against public salary data for comparable roles in civic tech and government services — including peer firms, federal labor-rate data, and published pay bands — then map those benchmarks to our years-of-experience bands. We target a single competitive number per level rather than a range, so two people at the same level with the same experience are always paid the same. The grid is reviewed annually: COLA adjustments apply across every level, and benchmarks are re-checked so the grid stays competitive as the market moves.

Sources we benchmark against:

  • Federal labor-rate (LCAT) data — GSA Contract-Awarded Labor Category (CALC) pricing data, which shows awarded hourly rates by labor category and experience level across GSA schedule contracts
  • Federal wage statistics — BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) for software developers, designers, and project management occupations, including percentile breakdowns by metro area
  • Federal pay scales — OPM GS pay tables, as a reference point for the government-side equivalents of our roles
  • Civic tech peer firms with published pay — posted salary bands from comparable firms, including Nava PBC, Ad Hoc, and Truss, and the GSA TTS (18F) handbook pay structure

These are assignments, not promotions. They do not change base salary. They unlock eligibility for the “Helps Focus Scale” bonus pillar.

Bonus Pillars

Bonuses are paid twice a year. The percentage of salary increases with level. Unused pool is surfaced company-wide each cycle.

Pillar 1 — Helps Focus Scale

Biggest leg up for contract leads, managers of people, and KPs.

  • Onboarding buddy program
  • Interviewing
  • Practice leadership

Pillar 2 — Impact

Impact is about what actually changed because you were on the work — outcomes delivered for users and clients, and contributions that help the business grow. It’s measured by results, not activity.

  • Outcomes shipped that users and clients can point to
  • Work that leads to expanded scope, renewals, or follow-on opportunities
  • Press / case studies that demonstrate the value of the work

Pillar 3 — Builds Brand & Client Relationships

  • Speaking, writing, demoing work
  • Stakeholder trust
  • Winning new work

Pillar 4 — Corporate Improvements

  • Org health
  • Process
  • Change management

E-1 Associate engineer 0–1 yr · $70k

Core: Completes clearly scoped tasks with direct guidance. Asks questions before getting stuck rather than after. Writes readable, tested code with support from a senior. Beginning to understand the difference between a government product and a commercial one — the user didn’t choose to be here.

Technical expectations: Comfortable in one language/framework. Writes and runs unit tests. Can open a PR, respond to review feedback, and ship with help.

Civic tech context: Learning to navigate legacy systems and constrained environments without frustration. Understands that “done” in government means accessible, secure, and documented.

Moving to E-2: Ships tasks reliably with minimal re-work. Starts unblocking themselves before escalating. Shows curiosity about the system beyond their assigned ticket.

E-2 Associate engineer 1–2 yrs · $80k

Core: Takes ownership of a small feature or bug domain. Investigates before escalating. Begins contributing to team rituals with something substantive to say. Understands the policy or program their code supports at a basic level.

Technical expectations: Writes clean, reviewable code with minimal guidance. Comfortable with CI/CD pipelines, basic debugging, and reading documentation for unfamiliar tools.

Civic tech context: Beginning to understand procurement and compliance constraints. Can explain what their feature does to a non-technical colleague.

Moving to E-3: Consistently delivers with low supervision. Has navigated at least one ambiguous or blocked situation and resolved it. Actively helps junior teammates when asked.

E-3 Software engineer I 2–4 yrs · $100k

Core: Works independently on medium-complexity features. Spots problems before they become incidents. Communicates status and blockers proactively. Participates in discovery — not just building what they’re handed, but asking whether it’s the right thing to build.

Technical expectations: Writes well-tested, maintainable code across the stack. Comfortable with accessibility requirements (WCAG), security basics, and FedRAMP-adjacent tooling. Reviews others’ PRs thoughtfully.

Civic tech context: Understands the program they’re supporting — who the end users are, what their constraints are. Flags usability or equity concerns when they see them, even when it’s not their job title.

Moving to E-4: Delivering consistently without supervision. Beginning to influence technical decisions, not just implement them. Mentors E-1/E-2 peers informally.

E-4 Software engineer I 4–6 yrs · $115k

Core: Delivers independently on significant pieces of the project. Proactively surfaces technical risk to the PM and team. Can run a sprint independently if the tech lead is out. Starting to build trust directly with government counterparts on technical matters.

Technical expectations: Strong in at least one domain (frontend, backend, infra, data). Can architect a small system and explain trade-offs. Comfortable in legacy codebases — not just greenfield.

Civic tech context: Comfortable navigating government stakeholder reviews, ATO processes, and change-management constraints. Understands the difference between what’s technically ideal and what’s deployable in a given environment.

Moving to E-5: Demonstrably influencing technical direction on the project. Client-facing without needing preparation. Has mentored a less-experienced engineer through a real problem.

E-5 Software engineer II 6–8 yrs · $130k

Core: Sets technical direction for a project or workstream. Translates requirements into architecture that accounts for government constraints: compliance, legacy integration, accessibility mandates. Trusted by the client as a technical authority. Actively shapes team practices, not just their own output.

Technical expectations: Deep expertise in at least one area, workable knowledge across the stack. Owns an entire technical domain on a project. Writes RFCs and architecture docs that other engineers can act on.

Civic tech context: Can run a technical kickoff with a new agency partner. Knows how to move quickly within government constraints. Has shipped something live on a production government system.

Moving to E-6: Has led a team through a technically complex or politically difficult project phase. Is sought out by PMs and designers — not just other engineers — for decisions.

E-6 Software engineer II 8–10 yrs · $145k

Core: Owns the technical health of an engagement end to end. Can hand off a project in a state another team can maintain. Runs pre-project technical discovery independently. Contributes to BD — credibly represents Focus’s engineering capabilities to a prospective client.

Technical expectations: Expert-level in their domain. Understands procurement well enough to scope technical work for a proposal. Sets the engineering quality bar for the project.

Civic tech context: Has navigated a program restructure, a contract transition, or a major agency stakeholder change without destabilizing the team. Builds continuity into everything — documentation, runbooks, handoffs.

Moving to E-7: Influence extends beyond the project — shaping how Focus engineers work company-wide. Has grown a junior or mid-level engineer in a meaningful, traceable way.

E-7 Staff engineer 10–12 yrs · $160k

Core: Influence spans multiple projects and shapes engineering culture at Focus. Defines standards — not just follows them. Spotted by clients as a rare engineer who understands both the technical and mission dimensions of the work. Helps write proposals, guides practice hiring, mentors staff-track engineers.

Technical expectations: Recognized internally and externally as an expert. Contributes to the field — writing, speaking, tooling, open source. Can evaluate a new technology domain quickly and form a defensible position.

Civic tech context: Understands the policy and procurement landscape well enough to advise leadership on strategic technical bets. Has a track record of projects that outlasted the engagement.

Moving to E-8: Is the person Focus calls first when a new engagement has a hard technical problem. Has shaped a practice area, not just a project.

E-8 Staff engineer 12–15 yrs · $175k

Core: One of the most senior technical voices in civic tech consulting. Shapes Focus’s engineering philosophy, hiring bar, and technical reputation. Clients request this person by name. Operates with full autonomy — sets their own agenda in service of the company’s goals.

Technical expectations: Definitional expertise. Produces work that raises the bar for the whole practice. Can make technology bets for the company and defend them to leadership, clients, and government partners.

Civic tech context: Deep relationships in the civic tech ecosystem. Helps position Focus in a changing government technology landscape — AI adoption, platform shifts, changing procurement models.

The bar: Would be recruited aggressively by Nava, USDS alums, or top civic tech players. Stays at Focus because of the mission, the autonomy, and the impact — not because they have no options.

Focus Non-Negotiables

These apply to every hire, all practices, all levels. One “no” ends the conversation.

Adaptability
Adjusts when ground shifts without losing forward momentum.
Ownership
Treats the outcome as theirs. Doesn’t wait to be told. Closes loops.
Mission alignment
Cares about the work itself. Makes mission-forward choices when no one’s watching.
Communication
Says the real thing, to the right audience, in the right setting. Clarity over jargon.
Collaboration
Makes the people around them better. Doesn’t hoard credit, context, or decisions.
Decency
Treats teammates, clients, and partners with respect — always. Brilliance is never a license to be corrosive.
Stakeholder navigation
Reads the room. Manages up, across, and down. Builds trust with people whose priorities differ.
Professionalism
Reliable, prepared, composed. Honors commitments. Represents Focus well in any room.
Conflict management
Pulls disagreement into the open early. Disagrees without damaging the relationship.
Lean / user-centered thinking
Anchors decisions on user value. Ships the smallest thing that proves the bet.
Curiosity
Asks better questions than the assignment requires. Follows threads on their own.
Growth mindset
Treats feedback as information, not threat. Sees being wrong as a starting point.
Builder orientation
Learns by doing. Has a track record of shipping things because building is how they think.
